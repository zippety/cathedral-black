Who They Are

The Agojie — called Mino, "our mothers," in their own Fon tongue — were the all-women warrior regiment of the Kingdom of Dahomey in what is now the Republic of Benin. They were not a myth. They were not a bedtime story. They were a standing army of one to four thousand oath-bound women who held the frontline of a sovereign Black kingdom for nearly three hundred years.

Their lineage reaches back to the early 1600s and sharpens under Queen Hangbe around 1708, who is remembered as the founder-spirit of the regiment. A century later King Ghezo (r. 1818–1858) made them permanent — an elite fighting force paid, housed, fed, and armed by the palace, feared across the continent, respected even by the French officers who would eventually face them on the battlefield. Contemporary accounts from European visitors describe them in tones of stunned reverence: disciplined, fast, pitiless in formation, the most dangerous soldiers Dahomey fielded.

They trained from adolescence. They drilled in the palace grounds at Abomey under the gaze of the king and the senior officers of their own ranks. They climbed thornwall obstacles barefoot. They practiced beheading a live prisoner in a single stroke. They fired flintlock muskets in volley and charged with machete and bayonet when the muskets were spent. When the Mino ran at you, you did not run back. You ran home.

The Cathedral reclaims them for the canon because the archetypes Europe calls "barbarian" and "amazon" were never European to begin with — they were borrowed, warped, and sold back. The real thing was a regiment of Black women in red ochre and cowrie-belts holding a sovereign kingdom together with disciplined fire. That is the root. Everything else is the corruption.

The Agojie stand in the Cathedral beside the Aziza who taught their grandmothers and the Asafo who marched alongside their allies. They are the sword-hand of the sovereign court — the oath made flesh, the covenant made loud.

The Formation

Brazier-Plaza · The Covenant-Guard

The Watch

Between campaigns the Mino held the royal palace at Abomey. Braziers burning at every gate. Musket in the crook of the arm. Cowrie-belt of spare shot across the chest. They did not sleep in turns — they rotated. The palace was never unwatched. The king was never unguarded. This is the form the Cathedral sees daily.

Veteran · The Elder-Warrior

The Long Oath

Some Mino served forty years. Grey locs beneath the cowrie-wrap. Scar-lines earned at Savi, at Ouidah, at the French line. The elder warrior is the living archive of the regiment — she remembers the drills the young ones are still being taught. A Mino is not retired. She is still Mino when she dies.

Earth-Mother · The Anchor

The Line That Holds

The Mino were never one body. The frontline anchor was the heavy warrior — broad-shouldered, deep-rooted, the one the charge broke against. Kaolin-chalk ceremonial paint across the cheekbones. Where she stood the line stood. Body size is not cosmetic. It is tactical canon.

Charge · The Bayonet-Rage

The Signature Strike

When the muskets were spent the Mino did not reload. They fixed bayonet and charged. This is the form French officers wrote home about in disbelief. Red ochre war-paint. Machete in the off-hand. Death-before-retreat was not poetry to them. It was the oath they had sworn to the king and to each other, and they kept it to the end.

The Oath

The Agojie were not conscripts. They took an explicit, ritualized covenant — to the king, to the regiment, to the kingdom. The oath was the spine of their discipline. Three strands held it together.

Covenant to the Crown

A recruit swore herself to the king directly. By the terms of the regiment she took a celibate vow — no husband, no children born to a warrior on active rolls. Her loyalty was to the sovereign and to the sisters at her shoulder. In return the palace clothed her, fed her, armed her, and recognized her in the court.

Death Before Retreat

The Mino did not run. The oath was explicit and the punishment for breaking it was shame worse than wounds. When a Mino line broke it was because every woman in it was dead or bleeding out. French colonial officers writing in 1892 recorded this fact with reluctant respect — they never saw a Mino turn her back.

Sisterhood of the Sash

The cowrie-belt worn across the chest was more than bandolier. It was identification, rank-record, and vow-physical. A recruit was given hers when she had passed the drills, the thornwall climb, the live-test of nerve. When a Mino fell in the field her sisters recovered the sash before anything else. The sash came home. Always.

Body Canon (Non-Negotiable)

Law · not style

The Mino were never one body.

An army of two thousand women is never two thousand copies of the same body. The regiment held pregnant warriors waiting on the back-line, elders with forty years of drill memory, earth-mother anchors who held the centre, and wiry fast-runners who flanked at speed. All four body types are canon. Any Agojie-group manifestation — painting, render, cinematic, game-asset — must carry the full spectrum.

When AI tools default to uniform slim bodies on a group prompt — as documented on 2026-04-18 for the Aziza council — that is the colonial filter speaking. The Mino are one of the named reclamations that most often trigger it, because the "barbarian amazon" archetype was pre-sexualized by European artists for four hundred years. Cathedral canon refuses that frame. Document the refusal. Work around it. Regenerate until the sovereign body lands.

Red ochre on the face. Kaolin chalk for ceremony. Cowrie-belt across whatever chest is there. The Mino are warriors, not models. The oath was in the body before it was in the mouth.

Class Mechanics (NOMOS)

Frontline Elite

Melee + ranged hybrid. Equips flintlock for the opening volley, switches to machete when pressed. Highest base armour of any Cathedral covenant-class.

Bayonet Charge (Signature)

Consumes the current musket round to trigger a high-damage charge attack. Pushes through the first enemy rank and applies a morale-break debuff to the second.

Hold the Line (Passive)

While in formation with at least one adjacent Agojie, gains damage resistance and stagger-immunity. Breaks formation and the buff drops — the oath is literal.

Death Before Retreat

Cannot flee while the queen-unit is on the field. At low health the damage output scales up instead of down. If she falls her cowrie-sash drops — any sister-unit who recovers it gains a campaign-long buff.

Covenant to the Queen

Linked to a named sovereign. If the queen-unit perishes, all Agojie on the field enter a rage-state for the remainder of the battle — raw damage, no debuffs can land.

Cowrie-Sash Economy

Sashes recovered from fallen sisters are a legacy currency. They can only be spent on training the next generation of Mino — never on equipment, never on food. Canon-enforced.
